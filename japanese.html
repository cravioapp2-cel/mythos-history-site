Origins & Timeline

Japanese mythology developed from native Shinto beliefs, later blending with Buddhism after its introduction from mainland Asia in the 6th century CE. Much of Japan's core mythology was first written down in the Kojiki (712 CE) and Nihon Shoki (720 CE), commissioned by the imperial court specifically to legitimize the emperor's claimed divine ancestry, tracing the imperial family directly back to the sun goddess Amaterasu.

Unlike many mythologies, Japan's imperial line claims unbroken descent from these mythological origins, making Japanese mythology unusually intertwined with real, ongoing national identity and the imperial institution, which still exists today.

The Creation Myth

According to the Kojiki, in the beginning the universe was a formless, oily mass, from which several primordial kami (deities) emerged. Eventually, the god Izanagi and goddess Izanami were tasked with creating solid land. Standing on the floating Ame-no-ukihashi (Heavenly Bridge), they stirred the ocean below with a jeweled spear, and the salty drops that fell from its tip hardened into the first island, Onogoro.

The couple descended to this island, married, and gave birth to the many islands of Japan and numerous kami. Tragically, Izanami died giving birth to the fire god Kagutsuchi, and a grieving Izanagi journeyed to the underworld (Yomi) to retrieve her, ultimately failing and fleeing after seeing her decayed form — a story with striking parallels to the Greek myth of Orpheus and Eurydice.

Major Kami (Deities)

  • Amaterasu — the sun goddess, most important deity in Shinto belief, said to be the direct ancestor of Japan's imperial family.
  • Susanoo — storm god and Amaterasu's brother, known for both destructive and heroic acts, including slaying the eight-headed serpent Yamata no Orochi.
  • Tsukuyomi — god of the moon, Amaterasu's brother, whose falling-out with her is said to explain why the sun and moon never share the sky.
  • Inari — kami of rice, agriculture, and prosperity, closely associated with fox spirits (kitsune), one of the most widely worshipped kami in Japan today.
  • Hachiman — kami of war and, historically, a divine protector of Japan and its warriors.

Heroes & Legendary Figures

Susanoo — beyond being a major kami, Susanoo is also treated heroically for defeating the monstrous eight-headed, eight-tailed serpent Yamata no Orochi by getting it drunk on sake before striking, discovering the legendary sword Kusanagi within one of its tails.

Emperor Jimmu — Japan's legendary first emperor, said to be a direct descendant of Amaterasu, traditionally dated to 660 BCE, blending myth and the foundation of Japan's historical imperial line.

Momotarō (Peach Boy) — a beloved folk hero born from a giant peach, who set out with animal companions to defeat a band of oni (demons) terrorizing the land, a story still widely told to children today.

Yokai & Mythical Creatures

  • Kitsune — fox spirits believed to grow more tails and power with age, capable of shapeshifting, often serving as messengers of the kami Inari.
  • Tengu — bird-like or long-nosed mountain spirits, associated with martial arts and mischief, sometimes considered protective, sometimes dangerous.
  • Oni — demon-like ogres, often depicted as large, horned, and fearsome, appearing in countless folktales as antagonists to be defeated.
  • Yuki-onna — the "Snow Woman," a spirit said to appear during snowstorms, sometimes benevolent, sometimes deadly to travelers.

Shinto Belief & Practice

Shinto, Japan's native spiritual tradition, centers on kami — a broad concept including gods, nature spirits, and revered ancestors, believed to inhabit mountains, rivers, trees, and shrines. Unlike many world religions, Shinto has no single founder, central sacred text, or strict doctrine — it emphasizes ritual purity, respect for nature, and harmony over rigid belief. Shrines (jinja), marked by the iconic torii gate, remain active places of worship throughout Japan, and Shinto coexists comfortably alongside Buddhism for most Japanese people, who often practice elements of both without contradiction.

Real Archaeological Evidence

Ise Grand Shrine, dedicated to Amaterasu, is considered Shinto's most sacred site and has been ritually rebuilt every 20 years for over a millennium as part of a practice called Shikinen Sengu, reflecting Shinto beliefs about renewal and impermanence. The Kojiki and Nihon Shoki themselves remain the primary textual evidence for early Japanese mythology, while archaeological finds like the bronze mirrors and haniwa clay figures from ancient burial mounds (kofun) provide physical evidence connecting Japan's early rulers to religious and mythological practice. Japan's National Museums and international collections, including the Metropolitan Museum of Art's open-access holdings, preserve related artifacts.

Modern Legacy

Shinto remains an actively practiced religion in Japan today, with millions visiting shrines annually, especially during New Year celebrations and major life events. Japanese mythology's influence on modern global pop culture is enormous — yokai, kami, and folklore figures appear constantly in anime, manga, and video games, introducing these ancient beliefs to audiences worldwide who may not realize their deep spiritual roots.

Fun Facts

Japan's Ise Grand Shrine has been ceremonially torn down and rebuilt every 20 years for over 1,300 years, as an act of spiritual renewal.
The legendary sword Kusanagi, said to be found inside the serpent Yamata no Orochi, is traditionally considered one of Japan's three sacred imperial regalia.
Kitsune (fox spirits) are said to gain an additional tail every hundred years, up to nine tails at their most powerful.
Japan's current emperor is often cited as heading the world's oldest continuous hereditary monarchy, tracing symbolic descent back to the mythological Emperor Jimmu.
