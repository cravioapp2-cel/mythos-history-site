Origins & Timeline

Hinduism is widely considered one of the oldest continuously practiced religions in the world, with roots tracing back over 3,500 years to the Vedic period in the Indian subcontinent (roughly 1500-500 BCE). Unlike many mythologies on this list, Hindu mythology isn't a "dead" belief system studied only historically — it remains a living, actively practiced religious and cultural tradition followed by over a billion people today, primarily in India and Nepal.

Hindu tradition developed through several major textual layers: the Vedas (the oldest sacred texts), the Upanishads (philosophical texts), and later the great epics — the Ramayana and Mahabharata — along with the Puranas, which contain much of the mythology most people associate with Hinduism today.

Creation & Cosmology

Hindu cosmology describes time as cyclical rather than linear, unfolding across immense cycles called kalpas, each lasting billions of years, with the universe repeatedly created, maintained, and destroyed. One widely known creation account describes the god Vishnu resting on the cosmic serpent Shesha, floating on the endless ocean of existence, from whose navel a lotus flower grows, containing the god Brahma, who then creates the universe.

This reflects a central concept in Hindu belief: the trimurti, or "three forms" — Brahma (the creator), Vishnu (the preserver), and Shiva (the destroyer/transformer) — representing the eternal cycle of creation, preservation, and destruction that governs all existence.

Major Gods

  • Brahma — the creator god, part of the trimurti, though far less widely worshipped in modern practice than Vishnu or Shiva.
  • Vishnu — the preserver, believed to descend to earth in various avatars (incarnations) whenever the world needs saving, including as Rama and Krishna.
  • Shiva — the destroyer and transformer, associated with both destruction and profound spiritual transformation, often depicted meditating or performing the cosmic dance of creation and destruction.
  • Ganesha — the elephant-headed son of Shiva and Parvati, remover of obstacles, one of the most widely worshipped and beloved deities in Hinduism.
  • Lakshmi — goddess of wealth, fortune, and prosperity, wife of Vishnu.
  • Saraswati — goddess of knowledge, music, and the arts.
  • Durga — a powerful warrior goddess, created by the combined power of the gods to defeat a buffalo demon no male god could overcome alone.

The Great Epics

The Ramayana — tells the story of Prince Rama (an avatar of Vishnu), his wife Sita, and loyal companion Hanuman, as Rama battles the demon king Ravana to rescue Sita after her abduction, a story celebrating duty, loyalty, and righteousness (dharma).

The Mahabharata — one of the longest epic poems ever written, centering on a great war between two branches of a royal family, the Pandavas and Kauravas. Within it lies the Bhagavad Gita, a philosophical dialogue between the warrior Arjuna and Krishna (an avatar of Vishnu acting as his charioteer), considered one of the most important philosophical and spiritual texts in Hindu tradition.

Hanuman — the monkey-god, a central figure in the Ramayana, revered for his unwavering devotion, loyalty, and immense strength.

Mythical Creatures

  • Naga — divine or semi-divine serpent beings, often associated with water, fertility, and protection.
  • Garuda — a massive eagle-like being, Vishnu's mount, and enemy of the Naga serpents.
  • Ravana — the powerful ten-headed demon king of the Ramayana, a complex villain who was also a great scholar and devotee of Shiva.
  • Rakshasas — a class of powerful, often malevolent supernatural beings appearing throughout Hindu mythology.

Worship Practices

Hindu worship (puja) can take place at home shrines or at temples (mandirs), often involving offerings of flowers, food, and incense to a chosen deity. Hinduism is notably diverse in practice — many Hindus focus devotion on a particular chosen deity (ishta-devata) while still respecting the broader pantheon. Major festivals, like Diwali (the festival of lights, celebrating the return of Rama and the victory of light over darkness) and Holi (the festival of colors), remain widely celebrated both in India and by Hindu communities worldwide.

Real Archaeological Evidence

Ancient temple complexes like Angkor Wat in Cambodia (originally built as a Hindu temple dedicated to Vishnu before later becoming Buddhist), the temples of Khajuraho, and countless temples across India provide extensive physical evidence of Hindu worship and artistry spanning centuries. The Indus Valley Civilization (c. 3300-1300 BCE) has also yielded artifacts some scholars connect to early proto-Hindu religious symbols, though this connection remains debated among historians. Museums including the National Museum of India and the Metropolitan Museum of Art's open-access collections house extensive sculptures and artifacts depicting Hindu deities.

Modern Legacy

Hinduism remains one of the world's major living religions, practiced by well over a billion people today. Its influence extends far beyond religious practice — yoga and meditation, now popular worldwide, have roots in Hindu (and Buddhist) spiritual tradition. Festivals like Diwali are increasingly celebrated and recognized globally, and Hindu mythology continues to inspire art, literature, and film both within India's massive entertainment industry and internationally.

Fun Facts

The Mahabharata is roughly ten times the combined length of the Iliad and Odyssey, making it one of the longest epic poems in world literature.
Angkor Wat, the largest religious monument in the world, was originally built as a Hindu temple before later becoming a Buddhist site.
Many yoga poses and meditation practices popular in gyms and wellness studios worldwide today have roots directly in Hindu spiritual tradition.
Ganesha is traditionally invoked at the beginning of new ventures and journeys, as the remover of obstacles, making him one of the most universally beloved Hindu deities.
